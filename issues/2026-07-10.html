TODAY'S LEAD
Iran hit four countries overnight. Wall Street bought chip stocks anyway
Iran's answer to Wednesday's US strikes didn't stay inside Iran. The Revolutionary Guard and Iran's army say they hit American assets in Bahrain, Kuwait and Qatar — a Patriot battery, a satellite site, fuel depots — then fired 10 ballistic missiles at Jordan's Al Azraq Air Base in a second wave, pulling a fourth country into the crossfire. Trump had already called the ceasefire "over" at the NATO summit in Ankara before the strikes resumed. Brent crude, which spiked 5.4% to $78.19 on Wednesday's escalation, eased back toward $77 by Thursday's close, and US stocks rallied on a chip-led rebound. Markets are still pricing this as noise the Gulf will absorb, not a war that spreads.
Why it matters: A truce that can't stay inside Iran's borders isn't holding — it's just quiet, and oil traders are the only ones still betting that's the same thing.
THE TAKEAWAY
Iran hitting four countries in 48 hours while oil gives back a third of its spike is the clearest sign yet that markets have stopped pricing this as an Iran-versus-US war and started pricing it as regional noise the Gulf will absorb. That's a reasonable bet right up until Trump follows through on his threat to hit Iran's Kharg Island export terminal — the one target that would actually prove the traders wrong.

MARKETS
Memory chips decouple from the war
The S&P 500 rose 0.81% Thursday to 7,543.64 and the Nasdaq Composite jumped 1.30% to 26,206.89, while the Dow added 139 points (0.27%) to 52,487.41 — a broad, chip-led rebound even as Iran's retaliation spread to four countries overnight. Micron led the charge, surging as much as 8.9% after unveiling a $3 billion US supply-chain investment tied to GlobalWafers' Texas wafer plant, with the rally juiced further by reports that SK Hynix's US IPO is oversubscribed — a signal institutions still want more of the AI-memory trade, not less. AMD jumped 7.2%, Broadcom gained 3.3%, and the semiconductor ETF SMH rose 5.2%. Sharp number: Brent crude gave back nearly a third of Wednesday's 5.4% spike, easing to around $77. Expect that split to hold — memory and logic chips rallying on their own fundamentals while everything else trades off the Gulf headlines — unless Trump follows through on threats against Iran's oil terminals.
BUSINESS STORY
Beating earnings doesn't buy grace anymore
Levi Strauss beat Q2 estimates across the board — adjusted EPS of $0.28 versus $0.24 expected, revenue up 8% to $1.56 billion, raised full-year guidance, even a 14% dividend hike — and its stock still fell as much as 6% in after-hours trading. Why: Q3 guidance calls for just 4%-5% growth, a sharp step-down from Q2's pace, and CFO Harmit Singh admitted "fluid" tariff policy makes it hard to bank any relief, so guidance still assumes 30% tariffs on Chinese goods and 20% on everything else. The lesson: in a market this nervous, the number that moves the stock isn't the one you just delivered — it's the one you're promising next, and deceleration reads as a warning no matter how strong the quarter behind it was.
