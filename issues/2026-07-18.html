The Lead
Kimi K3 just triggered Wall Street's second “DeepSeek moment”
Moonshot AI released Kimi K3 on Thursday — a 2.8-trillion-parameter open-weight model that benchmarks put behind only Anthropic's and OpenAI's very best, built at a fraction of frontier-lab spend. Wall Street's read was brutal: the Philadelphia Semiconductor Index fell as much as 5.7% Friday, pushing it more than 20% below its late-June record into a technical bear market, while Taiwan's benchmark dropped over 6% and Japan closed down 4%. Nvidia shares slid enough to briefly hand Apple the title of world's most valuable company. Goldman Sachs' read was blunt: the era of unlimited compute expansion may be ending.

Why it matters: If a capable model can be built for a fraction of the spend, the trillion-dollar AI infrastructure bet needs repricing — not just one stock.
The Takeaway
Eighteen months after DeepSeek's R1 first cracked the “more compute equals more moat” assumption, Moonshot just proved it wasn't a one-off. Labs spending hundreds of billions on data centres now have to explain why that spend still buys a moat at all.

🤖 AI & Tech
While Kimi K3 rattled markets, the funding and ownership questions never slowed down
Mira Murati's startup bet against one-size-fits-all AI: Thinking Machines released Inkling, a 975-billion-parameter open-weight model (41 billion active) trained on 45 trillion multimodal tokens. It's explicitly not the strongest model out there — the pitch is that enterprises fine-tune it cheaply via Tinker rather than rent a frontier lab's one-size-fits-all API.

OpenAI floated giving Washington a 5% equity stake worth $42.6 billion at its $852 billion valuation, with Sam Altman pitching an Alaska Permanent Fund-style vehicle every major US lab would pay into. The FT called talks conceptual — a real deal likely needs an act of Congress.

DeepSeek is raising again, a month after its last round — in talks for $1.5 billion at a $71 billion valuation, roughly 37% above its prior mark, while prepping a mainland IPO filing for as early as this year, targeting a 2027 listing.

Anthropic is in early talks with Samsung to build a custom Claude chip, evaluating its 2-nanometer process and packaging. Samsung already backed Anthropic's $65 billion raise alongside SK Hynix and Micron — a hedge against Nvidia dependence, reportedly paired with IPO prep for as early as October.

Fireworks AI raised $1.5 billion at a $17.5 billion valuation to help enterprises turn general-purpose models into ones trained on their own data — the week's largest round, and proof that “make the model useful for us specifically” still gets funded, chip selloff or not.
🌏 World & Malaysia
Iran strikes escalate, Texas floods again, Malaysia's economy beats the doubters
The US and Iran traded strikes for a sixth straight day, with Washington hitting bridges and infrastructure near Hormozgan and Iran retaliating against US allies across the Gulf; oil has jumped 10% this week on Strait of Hormuz fears.

Catastrophic flash floods killed two more people in Texas Hill Country, with up to 28 inches of rain in three days and the Guadalupe River cresting near 37 feet — a grim replay of the region's deadly flood risk.

Xi Jinping called for a global AI push as US export curbs squeeze China's chip access, pledging 5,000 AI training slots for developing nations — even as Kimi K3's release this week suggests Chinese labs are closing the gap regardless.

Malaysia's economy grew 5.8% in Q2, beating every economist's forecast and accelerating from Q1's 5.4% on strong manufacturing, tech exports and construction — though agriculture contracted and the economy minister warned against complacency.

Melaka's Pakatan Harapan bloc confirmed its exit from the state government, pulling five assemblymen into opposition after a bill allowing unelected assemblymen passed 23-5 — the standoff Anwar spent the week trying to defuse.

Negeri Sembilan heads to the polls on August 1 after a royal-crisis rupture with UMNO; Pakatan is contesting all 36 seats and targeting 20-plus, unfazed by reports of a forming BN-Perikatan pact.
📈 Markets
Netflix's beat-but-miss quarter erased a year of goodwill in one session
The S&P 500 closed Friday at 7,457.69, down 1.01%, and the Nasdaq Composite fell 1.4% to 25,520.24 — both indexes closing out a losing week, down 1.5% and 2.9% respectively, as the chip selloff dragged nearly everything with it.

Netflix told the story best: shares plunged 9% to a 52-week low even after beating on revenue, because Q3 guidance came in under Wall Street's $13 billion mark and the company said it would cut viewership-hours reporting to once a year starting 2027. The stock has now lost 44% since its June 2025 high — the password-sharing and ad-tier tailwinds that powered the last two years look spent.

The sharp number: the Philadelphia Semiconductor Index is now down more than 20% from its late-June record, officially bear-market territory. Expect next week's earnings — and every hyperscaler's capex commentary — to be read entirely through the Kimi K3 lens: does more spending still buy a moat?
