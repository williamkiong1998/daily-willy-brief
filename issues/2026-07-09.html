TODAY'S LEAD
Trump declares the Iran ceasefire over, then proves it
Trump told reporters at the NATO summit in Ankara that he believes the ceasefire with Iran is "over," hours after Iran struck commercial vessels in the Strait of Hormuz for the second time in three weeks. The US military answered with a fresh round of strikes overnight Wednesday, which Central Command said were meant "to further degrade [Iran's] ability to threaten freedom of navigation" through the strait. Brent crude settled up 5.43% at $78.19 a barrel, oil's sharpest one-day move since the war first broke out in February. Trump didn't fully close the door on diplomacy — envoys Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner are reportedly still pushing to negotiate a durable end to the conflict.
Why it matters: A truce that gets declared "over" twice in three weeks was never really holding — it was a pause with a countdown, and oil is now pricing a war with no visible off-ramp.
THE TAKEAWAY
Oil is up roughly 9% in two trading days while equities barely flinched, which tells you traders now expect this war to run long rather than treating each flare-up as a one-off shock. Whether Witkoff and Kushner's back channel survives a second night of strikes is the only thing standing between here and a fully reopened war.

MARKETS
Broadcom builds its own weather system
The S&P 500 slipped 0.28% Wednesday to 7,482.71 and the Dow dropped 576.76 points (1.09%) to 52,348.39 as oil-driven inflation fears spread, but the Nasdaq Composite actually edged up 0.2% to 25,870.65 — chips carried it. Apple committed over $30 billion to Broadcom for more than 15 billion US-made chips through at least 2031, with Broadcom pouring $1.5 billion into its Fort Collins, Colorado plant. Broadcom shares jumped nearly 5% on the news, and Nvidia added about 1% on reports of fresh Chinese demand for H200 chips — both shrugging off the geopolitical mood that hit hyperscalers instead: Alphabet (-1.4%), Microsoft (-1.4%) and Amazon (-1%) all fell on data-centre spending jitters. Sharp number: Brent's 5.43% single-day jump to $78.19 a barrel was oil's biggest move since February's war first broke out. Expect that split to persist — company-specific chip deals holding up while broad tech sells off on oil and rate risk — unless Wednesday's strikes provoke another round of Iranian retaliation.
PRODUCT CRAFT
Duolingo is choosing slower growth on purpose
Duolingo is guiding to roughly 11% bookings growth for 2026, well below the near-20% it says it could hit if it ran the business the old way. Instead it's moving pricier AI features, like video calls with its characters, into cheaper subscription tiers, and letting adjusted margins compress toward 25% and gross margin drift down toward 69% as internal AI costs rise. That's a deliberate trade: management is protecting roughly 20% daily-user growth over near-term monetisation, betting that a bigger, stickier user base compounds into more pricing power later than squeezing today's cohort would. The lesson for anyone building a subscription product: when a feature's cost curve is still falling, the mistake isn't pricing it too low — it's locking in today's unit economics before you know where they'll land.
