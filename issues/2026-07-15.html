The Lead
AI capex just wrote IBM's worst day in company history
IBM shares cratered 25% Tuesday to $217.05 — the steepest single-session drop in the company's history — after CEO Arvind Krishna warned that clients spent late June front-loading capex into AI-supply-constrained servers, storage and memory, pulling budget straight out of IBM's software and consulting lines to do it. The stock had closed at $290.23 the day before, itself already down from an all-time high of $329.23 set just six weeks earlier. It's the flip side of the AI boom everyone keeps pricing as pure upside: the same reallocation stuffing Nvidia's and Micron's order books is starving incumbents that don't sell the underlying hardware. IBM reports full earnings July 22.

Why it matters: AI capex isn't new money — it's a raid on somebody else's budget. Know whether you're the raider or the raided.
The Takeaway
AI infrastructure spending isn't incremental money — it's cannibalizing adjacent IT budgets in real time, and IBM just became the clearest evidence yet. If you sell into enterprise software or services, the question isn't whether AI helps you: it's whether it's already eating your customer's budget line.

🤖 AI & Tech
The AI trade is bifurcating — winners, losers, one big IOU
Nvidia rallied 4.1% to $211.80 after a US trade official confirmed the company has begun shipping H200 chips to China, even as officials called volumes "trivial." Morgan Stanley reiterated its overweight rating, citing "accelerating growth rates" with quarterly revenue nearing $100 billion. The China door is cracking open — just slowly.

GPT-5.6 went fully public on July 9 — OpenAI's new three-model family (Sol, Terra, Luna), delayed for a US government safety review before wide release. Sol is reportedly 54% more token-efficient on agentic coding than its predecessor; the staged rollout says as much about AI politics as the model does.

Anthropic is now AI's revenue leader, on track for a $10.9 billion Q2 and its first operating profit among frontier labs, after confidentially filing for an IPO on June 1 targeting a valuation near $1 trillion. Claude Code adoption is doing the heavy lifting.

OpenAI pitched Washington a 5% equity stake, worth roughly $42.6 billion at its $852 billion valuation and modelled on Alaska's oil-revenue sovereign fund. Sam Altman's move to defuse political scrutiny doubles as an admission: regulatory risk is now a line item too.

AWS put $1 billion into embedding AI engineers with customers, following near-identical bets from OpenAI and Anthropic. The read-through: model quality was never the adoption bottleneck. Integration is — and every hyperscaler now sells the fix as a service.

📈 Markets
IBM's warning tanked IBM, not the market
The S&P 500 closed Tuesday at 7,543.59 (+0.38%) and the Nasdaq Composite at 26,107.01 (+0.9%), both lifted by a cooler-than-expected June inflation print and a semiconductor rebound — the VanEck Semiconductor ETF jumped 2.5% a day after a broad chip sell-off.

IBM was the exception. Shares cratered 25% to $217.05, the worst single day in company history, after CEO Arvind Krishna told investors clients are diverting quarterly budgets into AI-supply-constrained servers, storage and memory — the same demand wave that sent Nvidia up 4.1% to $211.80 and its biggest hardware suppliers higher.

The sharp number: June CPI came in at 3.5%, matching a 2020 low — which is why stocks shrugged off both a naval blockade and a historic earnings warning in the same session. Expect more of this split-screen market this week as bank earnings continue and every enterprise-software name faces the question IBM just answered badly.
💡 Startup Story
The startup that pivoted because customers wouldn't stop asking
Omen AI raised a $31 million Series A this month to monitor liquid coolant inside AI data centres — but that wasn't the plan when the company was founded in 2024. Omen originally built fluid sensors for construction machinery, chasing a Caterpillar-dealership use case. Then those same dealerships started asking if Omen could do anything for the buildings themselves — HVAC, chip cooling, anything with fluid running through it. The team followed the question instead of defending the roadmap, and found a data-centre industry desperate to catch bacterial contamination in cooling loops before it forces a five-hour system flush. Total funding is now $41.5 million.

The lesson: your best market isn't always the one in your deck — it's the one your existing customers keep pointing at. Listen to what they ask for, not what you pitched them.
