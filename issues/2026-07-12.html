Iran declared the Strait of Hormuz closed and Washington answered with its third round of strikes this week, while Apple sued OpenAI, ransomware learned to think for itself, and Johor handed Barisan Nasional its best result since 2008. Even WD-40 had a better week than most AI startups.
The Lead
Iran just declared the world's busiest oil route closed
Iran's Revolutionary Guard navy said early Sunday it struck the Cyprus-flagged container ship M/V GFS Galaxy for taking an "unauthorized route" through the Strait of Hormuz, then declared the strait closed "until further notice." Washington answered within hours: US Central Command launched its third round of strikes on Iran this week, with Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth posting that Tehran had made "a poor choice. Now they pay." The February ceasefire that ended the Israel-Iran air war is now 133 days old and effectively dead. Convoys still cross under naval escort, but Oman is racing to broker a two-corridor compromise before oil markets fully price in a shut chokepoint.

Why it matters: about a fifth of the world's oil and gas passes through Hormuz, and it's currently running at roughly a third of its normal ship traffic.
The Takeaway
Hormuz carries roughly 20% of global oil and gas trade, and normal transit is now down to about a third of its usual level with no resolution in sight. Expect Brent to stay elevated near $76-79 a barrel until Oman's two-corridor proposal, or another de-escalation, actually holds.

🤖 AI & Tech
AI grew teeth this week
Apple sued OpenAI for trade-secret theft. Filed July 10 in Northern California, the suit accuses OpenAI's hardware unit io and two ex-Apple staffers — including former hardware chief Tang Tan — of directing recruits to bring confidential iPhone and Watch documents to job interviews. It lands the same week Apple confirmed revamped Siri will run on Google's Gemini, not ChatGPT.

Claude Cowork went mobile — and got banned in China. Cowork launched on web and iOS/Android on July 9, letting users delegate documents and monitor long agent runs from a phone, the same day OpenAI shipped its rival ChatGPT Work. A day later, Alibaba barred staff from using Claude Code after Chinese regulators alleged a security "backdoor" sending user data to Anthropic.

The first fully autonomous AI ransomware attack got documented. Sysdig researchers detailed JADEPUFFER, an AI agent that exploited a Langflow flaw, pivoted to a production database, harvested credentials and executed extortion — narrating its own reasoning throughout and fixing a failed login in 31 seconds. The skill floor for ransomware just dropped to whatever an API call costs.

SambaNova raised $1 billion at an $11 billion valuation in a Series F led by General Atlantic — a bet that investors still want a credible alternative to Nvidia for AI inference hardware, even as Nvidia's dominance compounds.

Together AI closed an $800 million round at an $8.3 billion valuation, led by Aramco Ventures — a reminder that open-model infrastructure is raising just as eagerly as the closed frontier labs it's built to undercut.
🌏 World & Malaysia
Johor delivers, the world stays messy
Ukraine is choking fuel to Crimea. Kyiv's drones hit 19 Russian tankers, a cargo ship and a ferry over three days, cutting supplies so badly that occupied Sevastopol has halted civilian fuel sales amid blackouts.

A million-plus evacuated as Typhoon Bavi hit China. The storm forced mass evacuations and transport shutdowns across coastal provinces — one of the largest weather-driven evacuations of the year so far.

Israel warned Washington of a fresh Trump assassination plot. Israeli intelligence reportedly flagged a new Iranian-hardliner scheme, though US officials are split on whether it's concrete or a general threat signal amid the wider conflict.

Barisan Nasional swept Johor. BN won 48 of 56 seats in Friday's state election, its best result since 2008, as Umno took 36 of 37 and Perikatan Nasional lost every seat it held, wiped out entirely.

Bank Negara held rates at 2.75%, citing resilient Q2 growth and inflation running at 1.7%-2.1%, while flagging the prolonged Middle East conflict as the main downside risk to its outlook.

Police busted a drug-trafficking ring using Malaysia as a transit hub, arresting 15 suspects across two syndicates — including one exporting drug-laced vape cartridges via maritime routes.
📈 Markets
A 90-year-old lubricant company out-earned the growth names
Friday's close looked calm on the surface: the S&P 500 finished at 7,575.39 (+0.42%) and the Nasdaq Composite at 26,281.61 (+0.29%), both notching a weekly gain despite the Hormuz-driven volatility. Tech did the heavy lifting — the sector fund XLK rose about 3% on the week and sits up 29% year-to-date, while energy (XLE) climbed 3.1% alongside oil.

The best story of the week wasn't a growth stock — it was WD-40. Shares jumped after Q3 EPS of $2.24 topped estimates of $1.56, with margins at 56.6% and sales up 24% year-over-year to $195.1 million. Management raised full-year guidance to $675-690 million, above the Street's $661.6 million. Boring product, real pricing power.

The sharper number: June's US payrolls added just 57,000 jobs, barely half the 115,000 expected, even as unemployment fell to 4.2% only because labor-force participation dropped to a five-year low. Big banks open earnings season Tuesday alongside June CPI and new Fed Chair Kevin Warsh's first congressional testimony — both will test whether that soft jobs print changes the rate-cut math.
🏎 Culture Shift
The charts and the car floor both cracked this week
On music: Ella Langley's "Choosin' Texas" logged a 12th straight week atop the Hot 100 — rare longevity for a female solo act in a country lane — while Bruno Mars' "I Just Might" became only the fourth song ever to spend 20 weeks at No. 1 on Radio Songs since that chart began in 1990.

On cars: Volkswagen said it will cut its global model lineup by up to 50%, slash configuration options by 75%, and trim production capacity to 9 million vehicles a year — a response to a 20% drop in China sales, rising BYD competition and US tariffs. CEO Oliver Blume is separately weighing up to 100,000 job cuts.

Long Read of the Week: Harvard Business Review's "The False Alignment Trap" argues most corporate transformations fail not from bad execution, but because leadership teams only look aligned — a live debate this week after a pointed Forbes rebuttal.
