The Lead
A fifth of the world's oil just became a war zone
The eight-week Iran ceasefire ended this week, not with a whimper. Trump declared it "over" on Wednesday, straight out of the NATO summit in Ankara, and the U.S. hit roughly 90 targets inside Iran. Tehran retaliated with strikes on U.S.-linked sites in Bahrain, Kuwait, Qatar, Jordan and Iraq. The fighting has paused for now under what officials call a shaky truce, with technical talks continuing. The real damage is showing up in shipping: tanker traffic through the Strait of Hormuz — the route for roughly a fifth of the world's oil and gas — has ground to a crawl, and Brent crude is up nearly 10% on the week.

Why it matters: a fifth of the world's oil transits one strait, and it's currently a war zone.
The Takeaway
The Strait of Hormuz carries about 20% of global oil and gas trade, and it's been disrupted for days with no clean resolution in sight. Expect the volatility to bleed into every other market story this week, especially anything chip- or shipping-adjacent.

🤖 AI & Tech
The frontier got crowded this week
Three frontier models, one week. OpenAI, xAI and Anthropic all shipped flagship models within days of each other — GPT-5.6 (Sol/Terra/Luna tiers), Grok 4.5 (a 1.5-trillion-parameter model xAI calls "Opus-class," priced at $2 per million input tokens) and Anthropic's refreshed lineup. The frontier gap just became a pricing war.

Norm AI raised $120 million at a $1.2 billion valuation, led by Khosla Ventures with Blackstone and Bain Capital Ventures joining in. Legal AI keeps pulling outsized funding — enterprises trust AI more with rules than with judgment calls.

China is quietly reopening the Nvidia tap. Beijing is weighing letting Alibaba, ByteDance and DeepSeek buy a capped batch — reportedly under 200,000 units — of Nvidia H200s, for training on public data only, with Huawei chips still first in line for inference. Rationing, not a retreat.

Microsoft built a $2.5 billion services arm just for AI rollouts. The new Frontier Company unit embeds 6,000 engineers and consultants inside client organizations to make deployments actually work. Amazon ($1 billion) and Meta are running the same playbook — the money has shifted from models to implementation.

Meta shipped Muse Spark 1.1, a multimodal, agentic coding model with a 1-million-token context window — important enough that Zuckerberg posted on X for the first time in three years to announce it.
🌏 World & Malaysia
Six things to know before your coffee's done
Ukraine can now build Patriot missiles at home. Trump granted Kyiv a manufacturing license for US-designed Patriot air-defense systems at the NATO summit — one of Ukraine's longest-standing asks, granted the same week he ended the Iran ceasefire.

Israel now controls nearly 70% of Gaza, up from 50% when October's ceasefire began. The UN says roughly 200 Palestinians have been killed near the shifting control lines since the truce started.

Congo's Ebola outbreak passed 600 deaths and is spreading beyond its Ituri epicenter into Tshopo and Haut-Uele provinces — now the third-largest Ebola outbreak on record.

Johor votes today. 2.7 million registered voters decide all 56 state assembly seats, with the Election Commission targeting 70% turnout. Pre-election projections had Barisan Nasional cruising toward a near-clean sweep.

MACC froze RM1.1 million and seized RM2.5 million in assets in a crackdown on a syndicate of enforcement officers and civil servants running an immigration-document racket involving 33 suspects.

Bursa Malaysia may list its first-ever video game publisher. CIB Development Bhd is seeking an ACE Market listing — small, but a symbolic sign Malaysia's gaming and creative-tech sector wants a public-market foothold.
📈 Markets
A memory-chip maker just out-IPO'd Alibaba
Friday capped a choppy week with modest gains: the S&P 500 rose 0.42% to 7,575.39, and the Nasdaq Composite climbed 0.29% to 26,281.61, both notching a weekly win despite the Iran-driven volatility. The Dow added 0.29% to 52,637.01.

The story of the week wasn't a tech stock — it was a debut. SK Hynix raised $26.5 billion in its US listing, the largest-ever Wall Street debut by a non-American company, eclipsing Alibaba's $25 billion 2014 IPO. Priced at $149, shares opened at $170 and closed up roughly 13%, after demand ran seven times the available shares. The reason: SK Hynix makes the high-bandwidth memory chips that feed Nvidia's GPUs, and AI infrastructure doesn't get a "Korea discount."

What to expect: Q2 earnings season opens next week, with JPMorgan, Goldman Sachs, Citigroup, Wells Fargo and Bank of America all reporting Tuesday, alongside June CPI and new Fed Chair Kevin Warsh's first congressional testimony.
