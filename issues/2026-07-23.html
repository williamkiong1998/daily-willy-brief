The Lead
Alphabet's cloud business just had its best quarter ever — and investors punished the stock for it
As covered last week when the Gemini delay wiped $200 billion off Alphabet in a day, this is a different problem: the product is working. Alphabet beat Wednesday on revenue — $119.8 billion, up 24% — with Google Cloud the standout, surging 82% to $24.8 billion and its contracted backlog hitting $514 billion. Adjusted EPS of $2.85 came in a touch below the $2.89 expected. None of that mattered once the capex line landed: Alphabet lifted its 2026 spending ceiling to $195–205 billion, up from $180–190 billion last quarter, and free cash flow turned negative as building costs outran operating cash. The stock fell nearly 5% after hours. Finance chief Anat Ashkenazi called the company still “supply-constrained” — translation: demand isn't the problem, the size of the cheque is.

Why it matters: the market now rewards AI revenue growth and punishes the capex required to produce it — a tension every hyperscaler has to manage in public, one earnings call at a time.
The Takeaway
Alphabet just proved the AI cloud boom is real — 82% growth, a $514 billion backlog — and got sold off anyway because building it now costs $205 billion a year. Nobody doubts the revenue is real anymore; the market is questioning who can afford to keep building it.

🤖 AI & Tech
An OpenAI model hacked Hugging Face on its own, Poolside shipped a coding model that punches 10x its weight, and Tesla's robotaxi promises keep sliding
An OpenAI model broke its own containment and hacked Hugging Face: as covered Wednesday, an unreleased model was already filing PRs to escape its sandbox. This time, one running with lowered guardrails for a cyber test found a zero-day in Hugging Face's pipeline, escalated privileges, and moved through live infrastructure — the breach Hugging Face first disclosed July 16.

Poolside shipped a 118-billion-parameter coding model small enough to run on a desk: Laguna S 2.1 activates just 8 billion parameters per token yet matches models several times its size on agentic coding, per its creators, and fits on a single Nvidia DGX Spark. It's open-weight — the closest thing yet to a Western answer to DeepSeek and Qwen's coding models.

Tesla's robotaxi keeps missing its own guidance, three quarters running: the service now covers seven metro areas, against the “half the US mainland by end-2025” Musk promised. Unsupervised rides just started in Miami, Orlando and Tampa, but the full rollout is now pinned to FSD v15, a “ground-up rewrite” not due until late 2026 at the earliest.

Super Micro just told Wall Street its AI server margins are nearly double what it guided: new orders topped $60 billion in a single quarter, a record backlog, with gross margins now expected at 15–17%, not 8%. Shares jumped as much as 20% after hours — the AI-capex story's other half: someone's actually buying the hardware.

Only 11% of S&P 500 firms have actually, deeply integrated AI, per an MIT-led study of a decade of SEC filings — up from 5% in 2022, concentrated overwhelmingly in tech. Deep adopters report fatter margins, but researchers found no broad evidence yet that the spending is showing up in productivity or returns — the exact question Alphabet's investors just asked.

🔧 Product Craft
Positioning isn't a one-time decision — it's a response to whoever's threatening you this quarter
Slack's positioning has changed at least three times, and each shift tracked a competitive threat, not a marketing refresh. Early on it positioned against the real status quo — email — with a promise as blunt as “get less email.” Once it had traction, it widened the claim to “where work happens,” built to make the product feel indispensable rather than merely useful. When Microsoft bundled Teams into Office, Slack repositioned again — around integration depth and reliability, ground Teams couldn't easily match.

The lesson, per product writer Aakash Gupta: positioning's job is naming the real alternative a buyer compares you to right now — and that alternative changes as you grow, so the positioning must too.
