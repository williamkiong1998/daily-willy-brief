The Lead
Iran's war just crossed from shipping disruption to a neighbour's oil fields
As covered through the weekend, the ceasefire framework that briefly paused the US-Iran war has now fully collapsed. Saturday's exchange showed exactly what that means: US strikes damaged tunnels and a bridge on the highway into Bandar Abbas and hit an electricity-and-desalination plant in Hormozgan, while Iran responded by striking Kuwaiti oil infrastructure directly — Kuwait Petroleum Corporation reported “severe material losses” and injuries. That's a first: Tehran hitting a neighbour's oil sector, not just shipping lanes or water plants. Trump has returned to threatening Iranian power stations and bridges to force Tehran's hand on Hormuz, through which a fifth of the world's oil and gas once flowed in peacetime. Brent crude closed Friday above $87, up more than 14% for the week.

Why it matters: Every strike that lands on energy infrastructure — anyone's — makes a prolonged Hormuz disruption look less like a risk scenario and more like the baseline.
The Takeaway
A ceasefire that's been reinstated once and collapsed twice isn't a diplomatic framework — it's a delay tactic. With Iran now hitting Gulf oil infrastructure directly, the market question has shifted from “will Hormuz close” to “how many of Iran's neighbours get pulled into the disruption too.”

🤖 AI & Tech
The defence-AI money keeps compounding, and Anthropic's shadow price just crossed a trillion
Chai Discovery tripled its valuation in seven months: the AI-molecular-design startup closed a $400 million Series C at $3.8 billion, with Eli Lilly, Pfizer and a fresh Novartis antibody-discovery deal already on its books. AI-native biotech is compounding faster than the model-layer hype cycle it sits underneath.

Shield AI's defence round dwarfs Europe's biggest: the autonomous-pilot startup raised $1.5 billion at a $12.7 billion valuation, plus $500 million in preferred equity from Blackstone — days after Munich's Helsing raised $1.8 billion. Autonomous-warfare software is now pulling nine and ten-figure checks on both sides of the Atlantic.

Anthropic's private price already implies a trillion-dollar debut: the company confidentially filed for an IPO in June at a $965 billion valuation, and secondary trading now prices it near or above $1 trillion — against roughly $47 billion in annualised revenue. A fall listing, if markets hold.

Claude Sonnet 5's discount has an expiry date: the $2/$10-per-million-token introductory pricing holds only through August 31 — after that it's $3/$15, and a new tokenizer can turn the same prompt into up to 35% more billable tokens. Budget accordingly before September.

Moonshot will fully open-source Kimi K3 by month's end: the 2.8-trillion-parameter model that triggered Friday's chip rout won't just be benchmarked-and-gone — free weights mean any lab, anywhere, can adapt the thing that spooked Wall Street. Second “DeepSeek moment,” open licence included.
🌏 World & Malaysia
Britain gets a new PM today, the Fed goes quiet, and Johor's data-centre boom lands next door
Andy Burnham takes office as Britain's seventh PM in a decade today, succeeding Keir Starmer. His first move: scrapping Starmer's £1.8 billion mandatory digital ID scheme to redirect the money toward cost-of-living relief.

The Fed's new chair just killed forward guidance: Kevin Warsh held rates steady and says he won't hint at future moves, arguing markets work better reacting to data than anticipating the Fed. Rate-hike odds swung from 18% to 36% in under two weeks anyway.

China's chip exports are booming while its own investment isn't: semiconductor exports jumped 110% year-on-year even as fixed-asset investment posted its steepest drop since 2020 — hardware demand red-hot, domestic capex cold.

Vertiv's first Southeast Asian factory just opened — in Johor: the US data-centre equipment maker's new plant builds power and cooling gear for hyperscalers regionwide, fully operational by 2027. Johor now holds roughly 80% of Malaysia's operational data-centre capacity.

Bursa shrugged off Wall Street's mood: the FBM KLCI closed 0.54% higher Friday at 1,731.45, lifted by foreign inflows and a stronger-than-expected Q2 GDP read, even as US markets logged their worst week in months.

Negeri Sembilan's campaign enters its final stretch: Anwar's Pakatan Harapan has two weeks to defend all 36 state seats before the Aug 1 vote — its first real test since Johor's landslide loss on July 11.
📈 Markets
Chips are in a bear market, Apple briefly wore the crown again, and nobody's cutting spending
The S&P 500 closed Friday at 7,457.69, down 1.01%, and the Nasdaq Composite fell 1.4% to 25,520.24 — both closing out their worst week in months, down 1.5% and 2.9% respectively, as the semiconductor selloff and Gulf-war risk competed for investors' attention.

The Philadelphia Semiconductor Index told the real story: it's now down 20.2% from its June 22 record, officially bear-market territory, after its steepest weekly slide since April 2025 — triggered by Moonshot's Kimi K3 release reviving DeepSeek-style fears about AI capex discipline. Nvidia briefly ceded the title of world's most valuable company to Apple intraday Friday, both hovering near $4.9 trillion, before clawing it back by the close.

The sharp number: even after the rout, the SOX is still up more than 60% this year — nearly seven times the S&P's roughly 9% gain. This reads like a correction inside a boom, not the boom ending.

Cashflow Lens (h/t @the_prosperityplan's discount-on-fear framing): Qualcomm, down with the rest of the sector on no news specific to the company, trades around a 2.1–2.5% forward yield — barely above its own 5-year average near 2.1%. Payout estimates run 30–38% of earnings, comfortably covered, and nothing about this selloff touches Qualcomm's cash generation. Read: this isn't a discount yet, just a stock riding its sector down — it gets genuinely interesting only if the yield stretches further from here.

What to expect: Alphabet and Tesla headline this week's earnings, both reporting Wednesday, among 77 S&P 500 companies due out.
