The Lead
The US-Iran war just entered its second week — oil is the tell
US Central Command completed a seventh straight night of strikes on Iran Saturday, hitting surveillance sites, weapons storage and an electricity-and-desalination plant in Hormozgan, while its naval blockade of Iranian ports holds. Iran hit back across the Gulf: Jordan intercepted ten Iranian missiles, and Kuwait says Iranian drones struck a desalination plant its people depend on for 90% of their drinking water — the second such strike in two days. Tehran has now suspended its commitments under the memorandum that briefly paused hostilities last month. Brent crude closed Friday at $88.09, up 4.6% on the day and more than 14% for the week, as roughly a fifth of the world's oil traffic backs up at the strait.

Why it matters: This stopped being a Middle East story a week ago — it's now a global shipping and inflation story, and every oil-importing economy is paying Tehran's war tax.
The Takeaway
A ceasefire that gets “reinstated” twice in a month isn't a ceasefire — it's a pause for reloading. With Iran now formally off the deal and a naval blockade in place, the real question isn't whether oil spikes further, but how high before demand destruction or a diplomatic circuit-breaker kicks in.

🤖 AI & Tech
Google stumbled, China's models keep landing punches, and the money kept moving
Google delayed its flagship model and lost $200 billion in a day: Alphabet shares fell 4% Thursday after Bloomberg reported Gemini 3.5 Pro — due in June — is months late because coding and long-horizon reasoning fell short internally. Google reportedly scrapped the original base model and restarted pretraining.

Trump's former AI czar says the US is “tying itself in knots”: David Sacks warned America risks losing the AI race after Kimi K3 took the top spot on the Frontend Code Arena within a day of launch — the first time a Chinese model has done so. His target: data-centre bans, state AI rules and proposed federal pre-approval for frontier models.

Nvidia's China chips are trickling back, not flooding: Nvidia resumed H20 sales to China after Washington's reversal and ordered 300,000 more units from TSMC — but a US trade official says “very few” have actually shipped, with Huawei and Cambricon eating into the share Nvidia is trying to reclaim.

Europe's biggest-ever defence-tech round: Munich's Helsing raised $1.8 billion at an $18 billion valuation, backed by Goldman Sachs, JPMorgan and Lightspeed, to keep scaling its autonomous strike drones and AI pilot software. Investor demand, the company said, “significantly exceeded” the allocation.

OpenAI's deployment arm went shopping again: The OpenAI Deployment Company agreed to acquire Northslope, a Palantir-rooted applied-AI firm whose revenue grew 7x last year — its second acquisition since launching in May with $4 billion earmarked for exactly this kind of buy.
🌏 World & Malaysia
Britain gets a new PM, the earth moved in Chiapas, and Padini's nightmare ends
Britain gets its seventh prime minister in a decade: Andy Burnham was confirmed Labour leader Friday after securing 379 of 403 MPs' nominations, taking over from Keir Starmer this Monday and promising to put the cost-of-living crisis “front and centre.”

A 7.4-magnitude earthquake shook three countries: The quake struck off Chiapas, Mexico, rattling Guatemala and El Salvador and triggering tsunami warnings within 300km of the epicentre; six million people felt moderate-to-strong shaking, though Mexico's president says early damage reports are light.

Ukraine's deadliest strikes inside Russia in two years: Overnight drone attacks on fuel depots and logistics hubs in the Moscow and Tambov regions killed at least eight people, including night-shift warehouse workers, and wounded over 60 — Zelensky says the sites supplied sanctioned drone components.

Negeri Sembilan's election lineup is set: All 103 nomination papers across 36 seats were accepted Saturday for the Aug 1 polls, with Pakatan Harapan and Barisan Nasional contesting every seat — menteri besar Aminuddin Harun switched to Linggi, and Umno's Mohamad Hasan seeks a seventh term in Rantau.

Padini's MACC nightmare is over: The fashion retailer's shares jumped as much as 16% Friday after the anti-corruption commission unfroze all its bank accounts, three months into a money-laundering probe that never produced a single charge against the company or its staff.

Penang landed a logistics win: Jabil launched its Intelligent Logistics Hub at Valdor Industrial Park, built by a PTT Synergy subsidiary — another sign Malaysia's electronics-manufacturing corridor keeps absorbing supply-chain investment as global firms diversify out of China.
📈 Markets
TSMC crushed its quarter and still lost 3.6% — that's how nervous this market is
The S&P 500 closed Friday at 7,457.69, down 1.01%, and the Nasdaq Composite fell 1.4% to 25,520.24 — both closing out a losing week as chip stocks and Middle East risk weighed at the same time.

TSMC told the week's real story: Taiwan's chipmaker posted a fifth straight record quarter, net income up 77.4% to NT$706.56 billion, and tacked on another $100 billion to its Arizona build-out, taking total US commitments to $265 billion. The stock still fell 3.64% Friday, dragged down with the rest of the sector by Kimi K3 jitters — beating estimates by that much and losing money on the day anyway tells you exactly how nervous this market is about AI capex.

The sharp number: Brent crude is up more than 14% this week alone. Expect Sunday evening's Globex reopening — the market's first chance to react to a war entering its second week — to set Monday's tone before a single earnings report does.
🏎 Culture Shift
A pop star went darker, Tesla beat estimates, and nobody's convinced either fixed the real problem
On music: Gracie Abrams released her third album, “Daughter From Hell,” a 16-track pivot into rawer, more dramatic territory that Rolling Stone called her best work yet — while The Guardian called the same record “bloodless.” Proof that ambition and likability don't always land the same way twice.

On cars: Tesla delivered 480,126 vehicles in Q2, up 25% year-on-year and well above estimates on a European demand rebound — but shareholders flooding this week's earnings Q&A aren't asking about the comeback. They're asking why robotaxi, Full Self-Driving and Optimus keep missing their own deadlines, with Wednesday's earnings call the next test.

Long Read of the Week: HBR's “Every Organization Has Two Realities” argues the company leaders think they run and the one employees actually experience are quietly diverging — and closing that gap, not redrawing the org chart, is the real leadership job.
