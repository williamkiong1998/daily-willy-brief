The Lead
The Iran war just got its first American casualties — and Washington is still escalating
As covered through last week, Iran's war had already reached a neighbour's oil fields; over the weekend it reached American lives directly. Two US soldiers were killed in an Iranian missile and drone strike on Jordan's Muwaffaq Salti Air Base Friday, with a third initially listed missing; a fourth died in northern Iraq days later while clearing an unexploded Iranian drone. CENTCOM now puts the war's toll at 17 killed and more than 430 wounded since strikes began February 28. Trump says he “feels very badly” about the deaths — and ordered another night of strikes anyway. US commercial crude inventories have fallen to just 43 days of supply, the lowest level in 45 years, and Houthi forces are now threatening a separate blockade on Saudi crude's Red Sea export route.

Why it matters: A war with an American body count is a war where de-escalating carries a political cost too — which is exactly how wars stop having off-ramps.
The Takeaway
Every week this war runs, the list of things it's allowed to touch gets longer — Gulf shipping, a neighbour's oil fields, now American lives directly. A commander-in-chief who says he “feels very badly” while ordering the next night's strikes isn't near an off-ramp; he's normalising the casualty rate.

Physical AI just got cheaper to prototype.

📈 Markets
Wall Street shrugged off Iran, and the chips that started last week's panic shrugged hardest of all
The S&P 500 closed Monday at 7,443.28, down 0.19%, and the Nasdaq Composite dipped just 0.05% to 25,508.07 — losses that pared through the session as Iran signalled openness to mediated talks, even with fresh US casualties on the wires. The Dow fell harder, down 307 points to 51,839.26, dragged mostly by one stock.

That stock was Apple, down roughly 2–3% and the Dow's single biggest weight, on a pincer of pre-earnings profit-taking and analyst warnings that AI-driven DRAM/NAND demand could cut ~190 basis points from margins — Apple's already raised Mac and iPad prices, with iPhone reportedly next.

The sharp number: the Philadelphia Semiconductor Index — the same gauge that fell into a bear market last week — jumped 2.37% to 11,950.11 Monday, with Micron up 5%, SanDisk up 6% and Nvidia up 2%. Last week's panic didn't survive the weekend intact.

What to expect: Alphabet and Tesla report Wednesday after the close, headlining ~400 companies due this week; Intel reports Thursday alongside the ECB's rate decision (84% odds of a hold at 2.25%).
🔧 Product Craft
Stop forcing the subscription, and retention goes up — not down
Elena Verna, Head of Growth at Lovable (formerly SurveyMonkey, Amplitude, Miro) noticed something conventional pricing wisdom said shouldn't matter: Lovable's users build in “streaks” — bursts of usage rather than steady monthly draw-down — so a rigid monthly plan created friction exactly when people needed more room to work. Lovable shipped credit top-ups alongside its existing subscriptions in under eight weeks. The received wisdom was that top-ups would cannibalise recurring revenue; instead, its highest-value customers bought the most top-ups, top-up repurchase rates matched or beat subscription renewal rates, and paid retention rose roughly 7% — “huge”, Verna says, on a subscriber base over a million. Lovable has touched its pricing more than ten times in its first year. The lesson: treat packaging as a live surface to keep testing, not a policy you set once and defend.
