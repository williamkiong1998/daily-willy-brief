The Lead
Iran and the US are now openly at war over the Strait of Hormuz
Iran's Revolutionary Guard navy declared the Strait of Hormuz closed "until further notice" early Sunday after setting the Cyprus-flagged container ship M/V GFS Galaxy ablaze — one of 23 crew members is still missing. Washington's answer was the heaviest bombardment of the week: US Central Command said it struck roughly 140 Iranian sites in a single night — missile and drone bases, naval assets, ammunition stores, coastal radar — part of a three-day total north of 300 targets. Iran retaliated with strikes on five Gulf states: Bahrain, Kuwait, Qatar, Jordan and Oman. The two governments can't even agree on the basic fact: CENTCOM insists the strait is open and traffic is flowing; Iranian officials posted Sunday that transit is "currently not possible." Trump's February ceasefire, declared over on July 8, is now just rhetoric.

Why it matters: about a fifth of the world's oil and gas moves through Hormuz — a market that can't survive both governments being right.
The Takeaway
Two governments are now issuing flatly contradictory statements about whether the world's most important oil chokepoint is even open, and neither has an incentive to blink first. Expect Brent to keep climbing until one side backs down or Oman brokers a face-saving corridor.
Both indexes gave back their Friday gains once futures opened lower Monday, pricing in the weekend's Iran escalation.
🤖 AI & Tech
Apple went to court, and the money kept moving
Apple sued OpenAI for trade-secret theft. Filed July 10 in Northern California, the suit accuses OpenAI's hardware arm — run by ex-Apple VP Tang Tan — of directing 400-plus former Apple staff to lift iPhone and silicon secrets; one hire allegedly stole a laptop and was coached to dodge exit security. OpenAI denies interest in "other companies' trade secrets."

GPT-5.6 finally shipped to everyone. OpenAI lifted its government-mandated access limits and rolled out the Sol/Terra/Luna family Thursday, ending a delay triggered by a Commerce Department review of the model's cyber-offense capabilities. OpenAI called the early-access period a "short-term step" it doesn't want to become the norm.

SambaNova raised its second $1 billion round in five months. The Series F, led by General Atlantic, values the AI-chip startup at $11 billion and lands alongside a new deal naming SambaNova an inference partner for JPMorgan Chase — proof the Nvidia-alternative trade still has believers.

Central banks are now openly worried about an AI bubble. Taiwan's central bank governor warned of bubble risk even as he called the underlying growth "real," days after the BIS flagged roughly $1 trillion in hyperscaler AI capex outrunning free cash flow and forcing new debt issuance.

Claude Cowork went fully mobile. Anthropic's agent-delegation tool expanded from desktop to web and iOS/Android, adding cloud tasks that keep running when your phone's off, plus new Microsoft 365 write access. Early usage data: only 8.7% of Cowork tasks are software development — the rest is ops and admin work.
🌏 World & Malaysia
Johor celebrates while the Gulf burns
Russia and Ukraine traded lethal strikes ahead of Paris peace talks. Russian drones killed four Ukrainians, including two in an industrial-facility strike in Kryvyi Rih; Kyiv's strikes on occupied Zaporizhzhia and Russia's Samara region killed five, hours before allied talks in Paris on pressuring Moscow.

Qatar's power-broker ex-Emir died at 74. Sheikh Hamad bin Khalifa Al Thani, who built Qatar into a regional mediator during his 1995-2013 rule, died as his country works to defuse the very Gulf crisis his diplomatic playbook was designed for.

A heatwave killed roughly 2,700 people across England and Wales. A new study attributes the excess deaths to May-June extreme heat — an early preview of the summer mortality toll now baked into a warming Europe.

Barisan Nasional swept Johor, and the state got a bonus holiday. BN won 48 of 56 seats Friday, its best result since 2008, wiping out Perikatan Nasional entirely. New Menteri Besar Onn Hafiz Ghazi declared today a special state public holiday to mark the win.

Bank Negara has now held rates steady for a full year. The OPR stays at 2.75%, unchanged since July 2025, as BNM weighs resilient domestic growth against Gulf-driven oil volatility as its main downside risk.

Syed Saddiq learns his fate this morning. The Federal Court rules today on the prosecution's final appeal against his 2025 acquittal in the RM10 million Armada funds case — reinstating the original conviction, or closing the book on it for good.
📈 Markets
A Korean chipmaker just pulled off Wall Street's biggest foreign IPO ever
Friday's session was the calm before this weekend's storm: the S&P 500 closed at 7,575.39 (+0.42%) and the Nasdaq Composite at 26,281.61 (+0.29%), both wrapping a decent week with the S&P sitting within half a percent of its record high. That calm didn't survive the weekend — futures point lower into Monday's open, with S&P 500 futures down about 0.3% and Nasdaq-100 futures off 0.5% as traders price in the Iran escalation, while Brent crude futures jumped 3.7% to $78.86 a barrel.

The story of the week was SK Hynix. The Korean memory-chip giant, which controls roughly 60% of the HBM market that feeds AI accelerators, raised $26.5 billion in the largest-ever foreign IPO on Wall Street, pricing at $149 an ADR and popping 14% on debut. It graduates from its temporary ticker to SKHY today.

The sharp number: June payrolls added just 57,000 jobs, barely half the roughly 115,000 expected. This week's test: June CPI on Tuesday, new Fed Chair Kevin Warsh's first congressional testimony, and big banks — JPMorgan, Goldman Sachs, Citigroup — kicking off Q2 earnings.
