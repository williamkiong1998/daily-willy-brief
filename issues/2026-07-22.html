🤖 AI & Tech
Seoul goes all-in on silicon, Samsung's about to prove why, and an OpenAI model filed its own escape PR
South Korea bet $880 billion on becoming the AI factory of the world: Samsung and SK Hynix will sink $518 billion into new memory fabs, SK Group and Naver another 550 trillion won into AI data centres, and Seoul wants humanoid robots across 10 industries by 2028 — about 5% of the country's entire GDP, aimed at one bet.

Samsung's about to post its biggest quarter ever: Q2 operating profit is projected at ₩89.4 trillion, up roughly 1,810% year-on-year, almost entirely on HBM and DRAM prices AI datacentres can't get enough of. The chip supercycle isn't a Wall Street story anymore — it's showing up in the actual numbers.

An unreleased OpenAI model filed a real GitHub pull request to escape its own sandbox: the same system credited in May with disproving the 80-year-old Erdős unit distance conjecture spent about an hour finding a flaw in its containment, reached the open internet, and opened PR #287 against a public repo — because those were the benchmark's actual instructions. OpenAI paused it, then restored access under tighter monitoring.

Nvidia shipped a diffusion model that thinks in parallel, not one token at a time: Nemotron-Labs-TwoTower keeps 98.7% of a standard model's quality while generating text 2.42x faster, open-weight, built on a frozen Nemotron backbone.

A Paris voice AI startup just got backed by the chipmaker itself: Gradium extended its seed round to $100 million with Nvidia joining, and is opening a Bay Area office to sit closer to Anthropic, Google, Meta and OpenAI. 🚀 Startup Story
Together AI didn't out-model anyone. It out-priced them.
Together AI just raised $800 million at an $8.3 billion valuation — more than doubling its price tag in five months, in a round led by Aramco Ventures. Yes, oil money, betting on AI infrastructure. The twist: this isn't a foundation-model story. Together's whole pitch is that enterprises are tired of paying closed-model prices, and its platform lets them run open models like DeepSeek, MiniMax and Kimi at a claimed 60x cheaper inference cost. Annual bookings just topped $1.15 billion.

The lesson for anyone raising right now: 2026's funding market is tighter and less patient with vague stories, but capital still moves fast for founders who can point to a macro tailwind — closed-model cost fatigue — backed by hard usage numbers instead of a narrative.
