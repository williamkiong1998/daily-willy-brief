TODAY'S LEAD
A three-week truce cracks over the Strait of Hormuz
Three tankers were struck by projectiles in the Strait of Hormuz on Tuesday, including a Qatari LNG carrier that Doha says was hit by an Iranian drone. Hours later, the US launched retaliatory strikes and revoked the sanctions waiver that had let Iran sell oil under the fragile truce brokered after February's war, which began when a US-Israeli strike killed Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei. The replacement license, "General License X1," cuts off fresh Iranian oil sales immediately, with only a grace window to July 17 for cargoes already in transit. Brent crude settled 3% higher at $74.16 a barrel and spiked a further 5.6% after hours to $76.04 — the clearest sign yet that traders think the truce is fraying, not holding.
Why it matters: A ceasefire that needs its sanctions waiver renewed like a subscription isn't peace, it's a lapse waiting to happen — and oil markets just started pricing the renewal risk back in.
THE TAKEAWAY
Roughly 20% of the world's oil traffic runs through the Strait of Hormuz, on a truce that has now taken damage twice in three weeks. Whether the July 17 license deadline produces de-escalation or another retaliatory round will set the tone for oil markets, and risk appetite generally, for the rest of the summer.

AI & TECH
Five things worth your attention
Together AI raised $800 million at an $8.3 billion valuation, more than double where it stood 16 months ago. Aramco Ventures led, with Nvidia, General Catalyst and Vista Equity Partners joining. The neocloud rents GPU capacity for open-weight models like DeepSeek and Kimi; bookings have passed $1 billion as enterprises keep defecting from closed APIs to cut inference costs.
A single report that DeepSeek is building its own AI chip knocked over an entire sector Tuesday: Nvidia slipped in premarket trading while Intel and Applied Materials both dropped 10%. The logic — if China's most-watched lab starts routing around Nvidia and Huawei both, the whole AI-compute demand story needs repricing.
OpenAI began a limited preview of GPT-5.6, a three-tier lineup — Sol, Terra and Luna — pairing a flagship with cheaper mid and entry options. Access is capped at roughly 20 partner organizations for now, after OpenAI shared the models with the US government first; a wider release is expected within weeks.
Brussels unveiled an AI-cybersecurity action plan days before the EU AI Act's toughest obligations — fines up to €15 million or 3% of global turnover — go live on August 2. Pairing enforcement with threat-detection funding is the Commission's hedge against being accused of regulating without building.
Ukraine wants AI models it can run without the vendor's permission, a government official said this week — sovereignty-by-design over dependence on any single provider's uptime or goodwill. For anyone selling AI into defence or critical infrastructure, that's fast becoming a baseline RFP requirement, not an edge case.

MARKETS
Chips sell the news, even good news
The S&P 500 slipped 0.45% Tuesday to 7,503.85 and the Nasdaq Composite fell 1.16% to 25,818.69, snapping Monday's record run. The real story was semiconductors: Samsung posted a blowout quarter — operating profit of $58.44 billion, up roughly 19-fold year-over-year and more than its combined profit over the past three years — and its stock still fell 8%, dragging the sector with it. Reports that DeepSeek is developing its own AI chip, paired with talk that Meta may resell excess compute, pushed investors to re-price AI chip demand broadly: Intel and Applied Materials both dropped 10%, AMD fell 8%, and the Philadelphia Semiconductor Index shed more than 5%. Sharp number: SK Hynix is still pricing its $29 billion Nasdaq offering into this selloff, the market's next real-time verdict on AI memory-chip appetite. Expect more volatility as oil's Hormuz-driven spike layers a second risk factor onto chip valuations already under scrutiny.
STARTUP STORY
The rarest exit in venture: giving the money back
Entropy, an a16z-backed crypto custody startup, is shutting down after four years, two rounds of layoffs, and a string of pivots — from decentralized custody to a Zapier-for-crypto automation platform nobody quite needed. Founder Tux Pacific's announcement was refreshingly blunt: no venture-scale business model, no growth story to spin, just an honest stop. What makes it notable isn't the failure — most startups fail — it's the $25 million check going back to investors, essentially the entire seed round Entropy raised in 2022. Harvard's Shikhar Ghosh found 75% of venture-backed startups never return a cent to backers, win or lose; capital that doesn't get deployed usually gets spent trying to justify itself instead. The lesson for any founder sitting on a pivot that isn't working: the fastest way to lose your investors' respect isn't shutting down — it's refusing to.
