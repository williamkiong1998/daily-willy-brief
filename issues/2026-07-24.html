The Lead
The ceasefire offer died quietly — Houthis hit two more tankers, and oil finally broke $100
As covered Wednesday, a ceasefire offer landed on Trump's desk hours after a tanker strike and a new Houthi blockade threat. Three days on, that offer looks abandoned: Houthi forces hit two more Saudi oil tankers in the Red Sea on Wednesday — the first attack since the blockade was declared — and Brent, at $91 two days earlier, blew through $100 on Thursday, first time since May, up roughly 7% in a session. Trump vowed to hit Iranian infrastructure “every time” a tanker is struck. The S&P 500 posted its worst day in a month, the Nasdaq fell 2.15%, and Asian futures opened red into Friday.

Why it matters: oil above $100 reprices freight, plastics, energy contracts and rate-cut assumptions everywhere at once — the ceasefire's collapse just became everyone's cost-of-goods problem, not just a Gulf story.
The Takeaway
The Iran war has now cost the US $37.5 billion and two de-escalation offers that both collapsed within days. Layer in Tesla's 14% capex-driven crash and Alphabet's buybacks going to zero, and Friday's real story is that AI spending and war-risk premiums just landed on the same balance sheets, in the same quarter.

Re-run your cost models; the next flagship is already in the oven.

OpenAI stops selling models, starts selling accountability: its new Presence product bundles guardrails, evals, simulations and Codex-driven fixes into a managed layer for enterprise agents handling billing, IT and claims. Raw model access was never the moat — the ops layer around it is.

A Chinese robotics startup goes unicorn on boring robots: Shanghai's Psibot, two years old, is closing near a $1.48 billion valuation backed by Chery Automobile and Lens Technology, betting logistics robots reach revenue faster than humanoid demos.

📈 Markets
Oil-shock jitters wiped out the week's gains, Tesla cratered on its own capex bill, and the Fed meets in five days
US stocks had their worst session in a month. The S&P 500 closed Thursday at 7,408.30, down 1.21%, and the Nasdaq fell 2.15% to 25,137.69, as the oil shock collided with fresh AI-capex jitters. Tesla was the story: shares plunged 14%, steepest drop since June 2025, after Q2 earnings missed and management guided full-year capex above $25 billion — more than double last year's spend. Alphabet slid on the same fear despite beating on revenue and cloud growth (as covered Thursday). Sharper number: Alphabet's Q2 buybacks fell to $0, from $13.24 billion a year ago, after raising 2026 capex guidance to $195–205 billion.

What to expect: the Fed meets July 28–29, rate decision Wednesday 2pm ET; Friday brings the US jobs report; next week lands Q2 GDP and PCE data, with Amazon due around July 30.

The Cashflow Lens: the selloff also caught IBM (-2.9%), a name income investors watch. Forward yield ticked to ~3.16% — still below its own 5-year average of roughly 4%, so this isn't the classic @the_prosperityplan discount-on-fear setup yet. Payout coverage reads mixed, ~58% on an operating basis versus ~88% against trailing GAAP — worth watching, not alarming. A real dip, a shallow reset.
🚀 Business Story
Apple beat Nvidia to the top by refusing to play Nvidia's game
On July 17, Apple's market cap edged past Nvidia's — $4.9 trillion to $4.8 trillion — capping a year where Apple stock climbed 22–23% while Nvidia limped to just 7%. The twist: Apple got there by barely spending on AI. Fiscal 2025 capex was $12.7 billion against $98.8 billion in free cash flow, a rounding error next to hyperscaler data-centre burn. Rather than build its own foundation model, Apple partnered with Google to rebuild Siri on Gemini and let its billion-device installed base monetise through services and lock-in. Spooked by AI capex with no clear payback, investors rewarded restraint over scale.

The lesson: you don't have to build the whole stack to win the platform. If your edge is distribution, renting the underlying AI can beat racing to build it — the moat is the interface, not the infrastructure underneath.
